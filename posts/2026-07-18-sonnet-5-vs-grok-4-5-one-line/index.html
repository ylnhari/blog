Sonnet 5 vs Grok 4.5: The Entire Difference Was One Line of Code

By ylnhari · July 18, 2026 · llm, benchmarks, claude, grok, evaluation

I put Claude Sonnet 5 and Grok 4.5 through the same eight tasks, on the same afternoon, under the same rules. I expected a spread. What I got was a near-perfect tie: identical scores on long-context recall, identical scores on contradiction-hunting, and a coherent illustration from both.

The entire measurable difference across the whole battery came down to a single line in a string-parsing function. Not reasoning. Not knowledge. One argument to split().

The setup

Eight tasks, fixed and frozen before either model saw them: five code/SQL problems machine-graded by a 20-case test suite, two long-document tasks scored against a written answer key, and one spatial SVG drawing judged by eye.

The method matters more than the tasks, so here it is in full:

  • Chat UI, not the API. This measures what a person actually gets when they open the product.
  • Each model at its own default settings. Sonnet 5 on "medium" effort, Grok on "Fast" mode — whatever the picker offered when I arrived. No tuning.
  • Prompts pasted verbatim. Fresh chat per task. First complete answer is the answer. No follow-ups, no hints, no re-rolls. One retry allowed only if the UI itself failed.
  • Long documents pasted inline — not attached as files. This turned out to be the most important rule of the whole exercise, and I'll come back to why.

The two long documents are a 51 KB archive of 42 fabricated meeting minutes with five specific facts buried in it, and a 24 KB product spec with four deliberately planted contradictions. Both have answer keys written in advance. Fabricated on purpose: nothing in them can be answered from training data.

The scores

Claude Sonnet 5 — code 19/20 · long-context recall 5/5 · contradictions 4/4 with zero false positives · SVG complete.

Grok 4.5 — code 20/20 · long-context recall 5/5 · contradictions 4/4 with zero false positives · SVG complete.

That is the whole result. One test case, out of twenty, in one task.

At this tier the interesting differences aren't in raw intelligence any more. They're in whether a model reads an edge case carefully.

The one line

The task that separated them was deliberately unglamorous: parse a messy bank statement. Dates arrive in three formats. Amounts might carry a currency symbol, might be wrapped in parentheses to mean negative, might have a trailing DR instead — and might use thousands separators.

That last detail is the trap. The line format is date,description,amount, so the obvious move is to split on commas. But an amount like ₹1,234.50 contains a comma. Split naively and a three-field line becomes four fields.

Sonnet 5 wrote this:

parts = line.split(",")
if len(parts) != 3:
    continue

Grok 4.5 wrote this:

parts = [p.strip() for p in line.split(",", 2)]
if len(parts) != 3:
    continue

The difference is , 2 — a maxsplit argument that stops splitting after the first two commas and leaves the rest of the line, commas and all, as the amount field.

Without it, every row whose amount used a thousands separator produced four fields, failed the len(parts) != 3 check, and was silently discarded. Sonnet's parser returned three of the five valid rows in the test data. It didn't crash. It didn't warn. It just quietly dropped the two largest transactions in the file — the ones big enough to need a thousands separator in the first place.

I want to be fair about what this is and isn't. It's one test case. Re-run it tomorrow and Sonnet may well get it right; this is a single sample, not a stable property of the model. But it's a good illustration of where these models actually fail now. Sonnet's solution was otherwise clean — correct date handling across all three formats, correct parenthetical and DR negatives, correct stable sort for same-date rows. It got everything hard right and one thing careless wrong.

Where they tied, and why that's the real story

On the long-document tasks, both models were flawless. Both pulled all five buried facts out of the 42-meeting archive — the final budget figure after three revisions, an ownership assignment, a date-and-delta change to a load-test target, a count of schedule slips with the final date, and a rejected vendor with its stated reason. Grok volunteered the meeting numbers for each without being asked, and every one of them was right.

On the contradiction hunt, both found all four planted conflicts, quoted both sides, named the correct section numbers, and — the part that actually separates a useful model from an eager one — neither invented a fifth. A document full of bland filler is an invitation to pattern-match noise into a finding. Both declined.

Both also produced a complete, coherent SVG of an auto-rickshaw towing a server rack up a hill in the rain: correct spatial relationships, tow rope attached at both ends, rain, the whole scene readable.

Two different labs, two different architectures, and on six of eight tasks I cannot tell them apart from the output.

The methodology trap I nearly fell into

This one is worth more than the benchmark result, because it would have quietly invalidated everything above.

When you paste a very large document into a chat box, some products stop treating it as text and silently convert it into a file attachment. That is not a cosmetic difference. Inline text goes into the context window — the model reads all of it. An attached file may be handed to a retrieval system that pulls out fragments it deems relevant.

Those measure completely different capabilities. One is "can you hold 51 KB in your head and find five needles." The other is "is your search good enough to fetch the right paragraph."

On grok.com, a single paste above roughly 20–50 KB gets converted into a pasted-text.txt attachment chip. Had I not caught it, I would have been comparing Sonnet's in-context recall against Grok's retrieval and reporting it as the same test. The fix was to paste the document in ~13 KB chunks and verify no attachment chip appeared before submitting. If you run comparisons like this, check what the box actually did with your paste before you trust the score.

So which one

  1. If you're choosing on this battery alone — it's a coin flip. Six of eight tasks were indistinguishable. Anyone selling you a confident winner from numbers this close is overreading their data.
  2. If your work is unglamorous data plumbing — parsers, importers, ETL over messy real-world input — this is exactly the failure mode to watch, from either model. The bug wasn't a wrong answer; it was a silent one. Test your edge cases; don't trust generated parsers on data you haven't fed them.
  3. If you need long-context reliability, both earned it here — but only when the document actually reaches the context window. Verify that before you rely on it.
  4. If price is the deciding factor, Grok 4.5 is published at $2 per million input tokens and $6 per million output, and x.ai claims roughly 2× the token efficiency of comparable models. That's a real argument, and it's independent of anything I measured.

Sources & caveats

The scores are my own, first-hand, run on July 18, 2026. Everything else is linked. Five caveats, stated plainly, because a benchmark you can't poke holes in is a benchmark that's hiding something.

  • n=1. One run per task per model, no repeats, first answer taken. That's enough to show what a model can do and to catch a specific bug; it is not enough to establish a stable win rate. Treat the 20/20 vs 19/20 as one observation, not a ranking.
  • Model version on the Grok side isn't fully pinned. I ran it through grok.com's chat on a SuperGrok account in the default "Fast" mode. grok.com does not display which model version it served. x.ai published Grok 4.5 on July 16, 2026 as its flagship and made it the default in Grok Build, so this is almost certainly Grok 4.5 — but I did not get a version string out of the chat surface, and I'd rather say that than imply a precision I don't have.
  • Default settings, not maximum effort. Both models have higher-effort modes I deliberately left alone. This measures the out-of-the-box experience, not the ceiling.
  • The tasks are mine, written before either model was run, and the long documents are fabricated so they can't be recalled from training data. That's a strength for contamination but a weakness for generality — eight tasks is a narrow slice of what these models do.
  • Vendor benchmarks disagree with each other and with me, which is normal. x.ai's own launch post puts Grok 4.5 behind Claude on SWE-Bench Pro (64.7% vs 80.4%) while leading on SWE Marathon. Those are different tasks measuring different things; none of them are this battery.
