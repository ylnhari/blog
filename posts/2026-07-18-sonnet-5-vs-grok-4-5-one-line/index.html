Sonnet 5 and Grok 4.5 Scored the Same. One Costs 2.5x More.

By ylnhari · July 18, 2026 · llm, benchmarks, claude, grok, cost-engineering

If you are choosing between frontier models on capability scores, you are using a tiebreaker that has stopped breaking ties.

I ran Claude Sonnet 5 and Grok 4.5 through the same eight tasks on the same afternoon under identical rules. They came out effectively level: same score on long-document recall, same score on contradiction-hunting, both drew a coherent illustration, and across twenty machine-graded test cases the difference was one. Not one task. One test case, traceable to a single argument in a single line of code.

Meanwhile the two models are not priced anything alike. Grok 4.5 bills $2 per million input tokens and $6 per million output. Claude Sonnet sits at $3 and $15. On the expensive side of the meter — output — that is 2.5x.

When two models produce the same answers, the only honest remaining question is what each one charges to produce them.

The evidence: six of eight are indistinguishable

The battery is eight tasks, frozen before either model saw them: five code and SQL problems graded by a twenty-case test suite, two long-document tasks scored against an answer key written in advance, and one spatial drawing task judged by eye. Both models ran in their own chat product at default settings, fresh session per task, prompt pasted verbatim, first complete answer taken.

Claude Sonnet 5 — code 19/20 · recall 5/5 · contradictions 4/4, no false positives · drawing complete.
Grok 4.5 — code 20/20 · recall 5/5 · contradictions 4/4, no false positives · drawing complete.

On the long-document tasks both were flawless. Both pulled all five buried facts out of a 51 KB archive of 42 meetings — a budget figure that had been revised three times, an ownership assignment, a dated change to a performance target, a count of schedule slips, a rejected vendor and the stated reason. On a spec seeded with four planted contradictions, both found all four, quoted each side, cited the right sections, and — the part that separates a useful model from an eager one — neither invented a fifth.

Two labs, two architectures, and on six of eight tasks I cannot tell the outputs apart.

The one difference, and why it is the interesting kind

The task that separated them was deliberately unglamorous: parse a messy bank statement where amounts may carry currency symbols, parentheses or a trailing DR for negatives — and thousands separators. That last detail is the trap. Lines are date,description,amount, so the obvious move is to split on commas. But ₹1,234.50 contains a comma.

Sonnet 5:

parts = line.split(",")

Grok 4.5:

parts = [p.strip() for p in line.split(",", 2)]

That , 2 stops splitting after the first two commas and leaves the rest of the line, commas intact, as the amount. Without it every row using a thousands separator produced four fields instead of three, failed the length check, and was silently discarded. Sonnet's parser returned three of five valid rows. It did not crash. It did not warn. It dropped the two largest transactions in the file — the ones large enough to need a separator in the first place.

Sonnet got everything genuinely hard right: three date formats, both negative conventions, a stable sort for same-date rows. It failed on carefulness, not capability. That is what differentiates frontier models now, and it is precisely the kind of difference a leaderboard number will never show you.

What this means for choosing

This is the third measurement in a row pointing the same direction. In Fable 5 versus GPT-5.6 the flagships split the board by category rather than one dominating. In Kimi K3 an open-weight model reached the frontier's top and immediately charged frontier prices. Now two models from different labs post the same score on the same tasks.

The pattern is convergence at the top. Which reframes the decision: when capability stops separating models, price and failure-mode stop being footnotes and become the whole choice. You can see the same thing on the cost-per-task map — the frontier is getting crowded horizontally, not vertically.

  1. Volume work where the outputs are long. Take the cheaper output token. At 2.5x, a workload burning ten million output tokens a month is a materially different bill for the same measured quality.
  2. Data plumbing — parsers, importers, ETL over messy real input. Neither model has earned your trust here. The failure was silent, and a silent parser bug is worse than a loud one. Feed it your ugliest real rows before shipping.
  3. Long-document work. Both earned it, with one condition: confirm the document actually reached the context window rather than being handed to a retrieval system. Products differ, and they do not always tell you.
  4. The last few points of capability. If your work genuinely lives where the top model is measurably better, pay for the top model — but verify that on your own tasks, because on these eight it did not show up.

Sources & caveats

The scores are my own, run 18 July 2026. Pricing is linked to source. Five caveats, because a benchmark you cannot poke holes in is hiding something.

  • One run per task, per model. First answer taken, no repeats. Enough to demonstrate a capability and catch a specific bug; not enough to establish a win rate. Read 20/20 versus 19/20 as one observation, not a ranking — re-run tomorrow and the parser bug may not reappear.
  • The Grok version is not fully pinned. I ran it through grok.com's chat on a SuperGrok account at default settings; the product does not display which model version it served. x.ai published Grok 4.5 on 16 July 2026 as its flagship, so this is almost certainly it — but I did not get a version string, and I would rather say so than imply precision I do not have.
  • Default settings, not maximum effort. Both have higher-effort modes I left alone. This is the out-of-the-box experience, not the ceiling.
  • Eight tasks is a narrow slice. They are mine, written before either model ran, with fabricated long documents so nothing can be recalled from training data. Good for contamination, limited for generality.
  • A measurement trap worth knowing. Some products silently convert a large paste into a file attachment. Inline text enters the context window; an attachment may be routed to retrieval instead. Those measure different things. grok.com does this above roughly 20–50 KB — I pasted in chunks and verified no attachment appeared, or this comparison would have been recall versus search reported as the same test.

A third column belongs here: Kimi K3, the open-weight model from the piece above, at a third price point again. Its chat surface returned capacity errors for the entire window, so it is not in this table yet. Same eight tasks, unchanged, when it is reachable — and the question that run answers is the one this post can only half-answer: across three price points, who actually earns their price?
