Claude Fable 5 vs GPT-5.5: The Honest Frontier Comparison

By ylnhari · July 13, 2026 · llm, benchmarks, claude, gpt, cost-engineering

Two frontier models now sit at the top of the market, and the internet has decided the comparison is simple: Claude Fable 5 is smarter, GPT-5.5 is cheaper, pick according to your budget.

That summary is wrong in a specific and expensive way. It is wrong because sticker price is not cost, because half the benchmark table is missing on purpose, and because the most consequential difference between these two models is not on any leaderboard — it is what happens when Fable 5 decides not to answer you at all.

This post is the comparison I wanted and could not find. Every number below is sourced. Where a number does not exist, I say so instead of guessing.

The two models, in one breath

Claude Fable 5 (claude-fable-5) is Anthropic's most capable widely released model, generally available since June 9, 2026. It has a 1M token context window by default, up to 128K output tokens, and costs $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output. Adaptive thinking is always on — you cannot disable it. It has a sibling, Claude Mythos 5, which is the same model without the safety classifiers, available only through Anthropic's invitation-only Project Glasswing.

GPT-5.5 (gpt-5.5) shipped on April 23, 2026. It has a 1,050,000 token context window, 128K max output, and costs $5 per million input tokens and $30 per million output, with cached input at $0.50. It comes in a second flavour, GPT-5.5 Pro, at $30 / $180 — a higher-accuracy tier for hard single-shot questions. Its knowledge cutoff is December 1, 2025.

So on paper: Fable 5 costs twice as much on input and 1.7x on output. Hold that thought — it survives about four more paragraphs.

Coding and agentic work: it is not close

This is the part everyone gets right, so let us get it out of the way. On every agentic coding benchmark where both vendors published a number, Fable 5 wins, and on the hard ones it wins by margins that are not subtle.

Two of these deserve more than a glance.

SWE-bench Pro: 80.3% versus 58.6%. A 21.7-point gap. SWE-bench Verified — the easier, more famous one — shows 95.0% against 82.6%, but both models are pressing against the ceiling there, and a saturated benchmark flatters the weaker model. Pro is where the daylight is.

FrontierCode Diamond: 29.3% versus 5.7%. Read that again. At the hardest tier of coding evaluation, at maximum reasoning effort, GPT-5.5 solves roughly one problem in eighteen and Fable 5 solves nearly one in three. This is not a percentage-point gap, it is a factor of five. Whatever FrontierCode Diamond is measuring, GPT-5.5 has barely started measuring it.

A benchmark near saturation hides the gap. A benchmark near zero reveals it. The frontier is defined by the problems models mostly fail, not the ones they mostly pass.

Where GPT-5.5 has the board to itself

Here is the part the "Claude wins everything" posts leave out. On four significant benchmarks, GPT-5.5 posted a number and Anthropic posted nothing at all.

ARC-AGI-2 at 85.0% is a genuinely striking abstract-reasoning result. GPQA Diamond at 93.6% is graduate-level science, effectively saturated. And MRCR v2 at 74.0% across the 512K-to-1M token range is the only hard evidence either vendor has published about whether a million-token context window actually works at a million tokens.

Anthropic did not publish a comparable long-context recall figure for Fable 5. Both models advertise a 1M window. Only one of them has shown you what happens when you fill it.

Absence of a number is not a loss. But it is not a win either, and you should be suspicious of any comparison — including the ones that favour the model I am about to recommend for most agentic work — that quietly drops the rows where its favourite has nothing to say.

Knowledge work, health, and the hallucination gap

On the benchmarks where both vendors did publish, Fable 5 leads on professional knowledge work by comfortable margins.

On GDPval-AA, an ELO-rated evaluation of real economic knowledge work, Fable 5 scores 1932 against GPT-5.5's 1769 — a 163-point ELO gap, which in any rated system is a different weight class rather than a close match.

But the number that should actually change your architecture is this one.

On AA-Omniscience, which measures how often a model answers confidently and wrongly rather than admitting it does not know, GPT-5.5 hallucinates at 85.5% and Fable 5 at 36.2%.

If you are building a retrieval system, a research agent, or anything that reports findings to a human who will act on them, this single number dominates the entire benchmark table. A model that is slightly less capable but knows when to stop is worth more than a model that is slightly more capable and cannot tell you which of its answers to trust. Everything else in this post is a tuning decision. This one is a design constraint.

Variants and intelligence levels

Both models let you dial reasoning effort, both offer five levels, and the levels do not correspond.

The asymmetry is the whole story. GPT-5.5's ladder starts at none — reasoning entirely off, the cheap and fast path. Fable 5 has no such setting: adaptive thinking is always on, and passing thinking: {"type": "disabled"} returns a 400 error. Meanwhile Fable 5 adds a max tier above xhigh that GPT-5.5 does not offer.

In practice this means the two models are optimised for opposite ends of the same axis. GPT-5.5 can degrade gracefully into a cheap classifier. Fable 5 cannot — its floor is still a thinking model, and you pay for that floor on every single call, including the trivial ones. If a meaningful share of your traffic is simple, that floor is a recurring tax.

On the variant question: GPT-5.5 Pro at $30 / $180 is not a general-purpose upgrade. It is a single-shot accuracy tier for the hardest questions in law, finance, and science, and at six times the output price of base GPT-5.5 it only makes sense when a wrong answer is far more expensive than the tokens. For agentic loops — where you will spend millions of output tokens across many turns — it is the wrong tool at any effort level.

Now the money

Straightforward enough — and this chart is where most comparisons stop. It should not, for two reasons.

Reason one: the long-context cliff

GPT-5.5's input discount comes with an expiry date measured in tokens. Past 272,000 input tokens, OpenAI bills the entire session at 2x input and 1.5x output. Fable 5 has no long-context surcharge.

Do the arithmetic and the coincidence is almost funny. GPT-5.5 at 2x input is $10 per million — exactly Fable 5's standard rate. Above 272K tokens, the input price advantage does not shrink. It vanishes completely.

This matters enormously for agentic work, because a long-horizon coding agent crosses 272K tokens routinely and then stays there for the rest of the run. If your workload is large-codebase analysis, document-heavy RAG, or any agent that accumulates context over many turns, you are operating on the right-hand side of that chart, where GPT-5.5's headline discount is a number that no longer applies to you.

Reason two: you are paying per success, not per token

Take a representative agentic coding task — 500K input tokens, 50K output. That is $7.50 on Fable 5 and $4.00 on GPT-5.5. GPT-5.5 wins by $3.50.

Except you do not want a task attempt. You want a task solved. On SWE-bench Pro, Fable 5 lands it 80.3% of the time and GPT-5.5 58.6% of the time. Retry until success and the real cost per shipped result is $9.34 on Fable 5 and $6.83 on GPT-5.5 — GPT-5.5 still wins, and anyone telling you the higher first-pass rate automatically pays for itself is doing motivated arithmetic.

But a failed attempt is not free. Somebody has to notice it failed, discard it, and rerun it.

The two lines cross at $5.45 per failed attempt — roughly eight minutes of an engineer's time. Below that, GPT-5.5 is genuinely cheaper and you should use it. Above it, Fable 5 is cheaper despite costing nearly twice as much per token, because it wastes less of the expensive resource in the loop, which is not the tokens.

So the real question is not "which model is cheaper." It is: does a human look at each attempt? If a person reviews every agent run, failures cost far more than $5.45 and Fable 5 wins on cost outright. If failures are caught automatically by a test suite and silently retried for pennies, GPT-5.5 wins on cost and the benchmark gap is something you are buying for reasons other than money.

The refusal tax — the difference nobody benchmarks

Here is the thing that will actually bite you in production, and it appears on no leaderboard.

Fable 5 ships with safety classifiers that can decline a request outright. When that happens, the Messages API does not raise an error — it returns a perfectly successful HTTP 200 with stop_reason: "refusal" and an empty content array. Code that reaches straight for response.content[0].text will break, and it will break in production, on a 200.

response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-fable-5",
    max_tokens=4096,
    messages=[...],
)

# Check stop_reason BEFORE reading content — a refusal is a 200, not an exception.
if response.stop_reason == "refusal":
    handle_refusal(response.stop_details)   # content is empty or partial
else:
    print(response.content[0].text)

Anthropic's own answer to this is a fallback: retry the refused request on Claude Opus 4.8, either by passing a fallbacks parameter and letting the API do it server-side, or by handling it client-side. Refusals that occur before any output is generated are not billed, and a "fallback credit" mechanism refunds the prompt-cache cost of switching models so you do not pay it twice.

All of which is a thoughtful, well-engineered solution to a problem GPT-5.5 users simply do not have. The practical consequences are worth stating plainly:

  • Your effective model is not one model. If you deploy Fable 5, you are deploying a Fable-5-plus-Opus-4.8 system, and your worst-case quality is Opus 4.8's quality, not Fable 5's.
  • The security and life-sciences false-positive problem is real. The classifiers target cyber and bio content. Legitimate adjacent work — security tooling, defensive research, genomics — can trip them.
  • Fable 5 mandates 30-day data retention and is unavailable under zero-data-retention. If your compliance posture requires ZDR, the decision has been made for you: every Fable 5 request returns a 400, and the model is simply off the table.

That last bullet is the one to check first, before any benchmark. It is not a preference. It is a gate.

So which one

Stop asking which model is better and answer three questions about your own system.

  1. Does a human review each attempt? If yes, failures are expensive, and Fable 5's higher first-pass rate pays for its higher token price. If failures are caught by CI and retried for free, GPT-5.5's cheaper tokens win.
  2. Do your prompts routinely exceed 272K tokens? If yes, GPT-5.5's input discount does not apply to you, and you are comparing $10 against $10 while giving up 21.7 points of SWE-bench Pro.
  3. Can you tolerate a refusal, and can you live with 30-day retention? If not, Fable 5 is not a candidate regardless of how good it is.

Everything else falls out of those answers. Long-horizon agentic coding with human review: Fable 5, and it is not close. High-volume, automatically-verified, short-context work: GPT-5.5, whose reasoning_effort: none tier has no Fable 5 equivalent and whose batch pricing at $2.50 / $15 undercuts everything on the board. Anything where a confident wrong answer causes harm: the 36% versus 86% hallucination gap should decide it for you before you read another benchmark.

Reading the numbers honestly

A few caveats I would want if I were reading this instead of writing it.

  • Several of Fable 5's published wins — Terminal-Bench 2.1, Humanity's Last Exam, HealthBench — carry asterisks in the source material relating to how Fable 5 and Mythos 5 figures are reported, and to safeguard routing. Treat the margins as directional, not exact.
  • Terminal-Bench appears twice at different versions. GPT-5.5 leads Terminal-Bench 2.0 at 82.7%; Fable 5 leads Terminal-Bench 2.1 at 88.0% to 83.4%. These are different tests, and quoting only the one that suits your argument is the oldest trick in benchmark journalism.
  • Vendor-published benchmarks are marketing artifacts. The gaps here are large enough to survive a lot of methodological doubt, but not all of them are.
  • The cost model above assumes a 500K-in / 50K-out task and ignores prompt caching, which both vendors offer and which will shift the absolute numbers — though not the shape of the break-even curve.

The frontier is genuinely close on raw intelligence, and further apart than the leaderboards suggest on the things that determine whether a system works: knowing when to shut up, staying cheap when the context gets long, and answering at all.
